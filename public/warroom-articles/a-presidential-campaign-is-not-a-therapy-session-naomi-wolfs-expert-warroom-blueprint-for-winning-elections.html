“A Presidential Campaign Is Not a Therapy Session”: Naomi Wolf’s Expert WarRoom Blueprint for Winning Elections

By KARI DONOVAN | 2025-03-13

In a Saturday discussion with Royce White on the War Room, Dr. Naomi Wolf shared her strategic insights on how to build winning coalitions in American politics. With a storied career advising Democrat Presidential Candidate Al Gore and other prominent Democrats, Wolf’s advice is grounded in experience and a deep understanding of the electoral landscape.

Wolf emphasized that a successful campaign requires more than just appealing to the base; it necessitates building broad coalitions that can win over swing voters in key battleground states.

Reflecting on the 2024 electoral dynamics, she stated, “We’ve gone from a sinking ship situation to a victory-ready situation, if the principles take a few simple steps between now and November.”

According to Wolf, the path to victory is not about catering to every demand or wish of the electorate but about strategically aligning with those who can actually deliver the win.

One of the most significant pieces of advice Wolf offered was the importance of forming alliances with unexpected partners. She pointed to the recent coalition-building efforts between Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and former President Donald Trump as a critical move. “Without RFK Jr. joining forces with President Trump… there wasn’t the math, in my view, to win, even if all the base turned out,” Wolf explained. She highlighted that this alliance could bring in disaffected Democrats, independents, and swing voters, especially suburban white women who are fiscally conservative and socially liberal—a crucial demographic in battleground states.

Wolf’s strategic approach is not just theoretical; it is based on her experience advising Al Gore during the 2000 presidential campaign.

She noted, “A presidential campaign is not a therapy session. A winning presidential campaign is not a Christmas wishlist where you get every single thing you want.”

Instead, Wolf argued that successful campaigns focus on pragmatic goals and build coalitions that unify diverse groups under a common cause. This requires compromise and a willingness to prioritize broader electoral success over narrow ideological purity.

Throughout the conversation, Wolf stressed the need for Republicans, particularly those supporting Trump, to expand their appeal beyond their traditional base. She urged them to “dial down the language that sounds homophobic” and to embrace a more inclusive message that can attract voters from across the political spectrum.

“If we get two to three percent of the environmentalists, two to three percent of the black voters, two to three percent of the feminists, two to three percent of the LGBTQ community, we got that coalition,” White agreed and stated emphatically.

Wolf’s point is clear: winning elections is about adding, not subtracting, and creating a coalition that reflects the diversity of the American electorate.

Wolf also touched on the importance of addressing issues that resonate broadly, such as public health and environmentalism, in a way that transcends partisan divides. She praised RFK Jr. for bringing attention to the country’s health crisis and criticized how public health has been fragmented by identity politics. “Public health itself has been subjected to identity politics,” she observed, advocating for a more holistic approach that addresses the needs of all Americans.

Ultimately, Naomi Wolf’s advice to those seeking electoral success is to focus on building broad, inclusive coalitions that can appeal to a wide range of voters. Her experience and insights serve as a reminder that winning a presidential campaign is about finding common ground and reaching out to those who may not fit neatly into any one ideological box.

“A winning presidential campaign is about reaching out to what unifies us as Americans,” she concluded, encapsulating her vision for a more united and strategic approach to politics.
